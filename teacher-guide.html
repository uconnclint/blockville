🍎 GROWN-UPS' CORNER

Blockville — Teacher Guide & Standards

A free, no-login voxel city-building sandbox for young builders. Kids draw roads, place buildings from a big picture menu, and watch a living city grow on a world of oceans, forests, and mountains — while practicing planning, community thinking, and caring for the environment.

👧 Ages 5–9 (K–Grade 4) 💻 CSTA 1A & 1B 🌎 NGSS science tie-in 🖨️ Print-friendly ⏱️ 20–30 min lessons

Tip: use your browser's Print (Ctrl/⌘-P) to make a clean handout.

1 What Is Blockville?

Blockville is a gentle, open-ended sandbox — there is no score, no money, and no way to lose. Children build whatever city they imagine on a big, procedurally generated island that is different every time. It runs in any browser on Chromebooks and iPads with nothing to install. Below is every tool and mechanic a student actually uses, tied to the real in-game action.

🎚️ Three play modes (chosen on the start screen)

🧸

Picture Play (Ages 5–6)

A simplified toolbar of six big picture buttons with read-aloud labels and almost no text — ideal for pre-readers.

🏙️

City Explorer (Ages 7–9)

The full building catalog plus the guided "City Helper" missions and the people/jobs/ happiness/clean-air stats.

✨

Everything

Every tool unlocked with no guardrails — for confident builders and free exploration.

🔊

Read-aloud & accessibility

A speaker toggle reads tool and mission names aloud; the whole city map is keyboard-navigable (arrow keys move a cursor, Enter places, Esc returns to Move).

🧰 The building tools (bottom toolbar)

✋

Move

The default hand tool — drag to look around the city, pinch or scroll to zoom. Nothing is placed, so kids can explore safely.

🛣️

Road

Tap and drag to draw a road. Drags snap to a straight line, and a road drawn onto water automatically becomes a bridge.

🏠

Homes / Shops / Factories

Tap a category to open a drawer of buildings; tap a card, then tap the map to place it. Buildings turn to face the nearest road.

🎡

Fun / 🏙️ Downtown / 🌼 Deco

Parks, schools, fire stations, rides, skyscrapers, and decorations — 93 building types in all, some 2×2 tiles big.

🌳

Tree

Plant trees anywhere on grass — they add greenery and help keep the city's air clean near factories.

🧹

Erase

Remove any building, road, or tree. A friendly red outline appears if a spot won't work, with a reason ("This building needs grass").

🌱 Living world & gentle cause-and-effect

🧭

City Helper missions

Optional bite-size goals with a picture and "2 of 3" progress — build a road with 5 pieces, add 3 homes, put a shop near the homes, build a bridge, add a park… A Free Build button opts out anytime.

📊

People, Jobs, Happiness, Clean-Air

Homes bring people; shops/factories add jobs; parks and schools raise the happiness face; factories lower clean-air unless trees or wind turbines are nearby.

⛰️

Generated terrain

Every new city sits on a unique island with oceans, lakes, rivers, forests, and snow-capped mountains to build around.

🚗

It comes alive

Cars drive the roads, people stroll, boats sail, factories puff smoke, day turns to night, and the seasons change.

🤝

Build together (multiplayer)

Tap 🤝 for a room code (like SUNNY-TIGER); classmates who enter it build in the same city together, live.

📷

Keepsakes

Name the city and snap a 📷 photo postcard, collect stickers for every building type tried, and keep several saved cities.

2 Standards Alignment

Blockville is a computational-thinking and design sandbox with a strong community & environment theme. Codes and titles below are quoted from the CSTA K-12 Computer Science Standards (2017) and Next Generation Science Standards (NGSS). Only standards with a concrete in-game justification are listed.

CSTA Level 1A — Grades K–2 (Picture Play / early City Explorer)
CodeStandardHow Blockville addresses it
1A-AP-08 Model daily processes by creating and following algorithms (sets of step-by-step instructions) to complete tasks. The City Helper walks children through an ordered sequence — road → homes → shop → bridge — and they follow those steps to grow a working town.
1A-AP-11 Decompose (break down) the steps needed to solve a problem into a precise sequence of instructions. Building a city means breaking a big goal ("make a neighborhood") into small placements: draw roads first, then homes beside them, then a park.
1A-AP-12 Develop plans that describe a program's sequence of events, goals, and expected outcomes. Before placing, kids decide where roads and buildings should go and predict what will happen — e.g., "if I add homes, more people will come."
1A-AP-14 Debug (identify and fix) errors in an algorithm or program that includes sequences and simple loops. The red "won't fit" outline and the Erase & Undo tools let children notice a mistake and fix it — a first, concrete taste of debugging.
1A-CS-01 Select and operate appropriate software to perform a variety of tasks, and recognize that users have different needs and preferences for the technology they use. Choosing Picture Play vs. City Explorer, and toggling read-aloud or Always-Bright, shows that people pick settings that fit their own needs.
1A-IC-17 Work respectfully and responsibly with others online. The 🤝 shared-room multiplayer has children build one city together, practicing turn-taking and respect for each other's work.
CSTA Level 1B — Grades 3–5 (City Explorer / Everything)
CodeStandardHow Blockville addresses it
1B-AP-08 Compare and refine multiple algorithms for the same task and determine which is the most appropriate. There are many ways to lay out the same city; students compare a grid vs. a winding-road plan and refine the one that connects buildings best.
1B-AP-11 Decompose (break down) problems into smaller, manageable subproblems to facilitate the program development process. A "capital city" is broken into subproblems — the housing area, the downtown, the roads that connect them, the park where people relax.
1B-AP-12 Modify, remix, or incorporate portions of an existing program into one's own work, to develop something new or add more advanced features. Students load a saved city (or a friend's shared room) and remix it — extending roads and adding downtown towers to make something new.
1B-AP-13 Use an iterative process to plan the development of a program by including others' perspectives and considering user preferences. In multiplayer, partners plan together, react to each other's additions, and revise the shared city as it grows.
1B-AP-15 Test and debug (identify and fix errors) a program or algorithm to ensure it runs as intended. Kids "test" a layout by watching whether the clean-air and happiness meters respond, then fix problems (add trees near a smoky factory) until it works.
1B-IC-20 Seek diverse perspectives for the purpose of improving computational artifacts. Building a shared city invites classmates' different ideas about where things belong, improving the final design.

3 Three Ready-to-Run Lessons

Each fits in a 20–30 minute block. Steps name the exact in-game tools. Choose the mode that matches your group (Picture Play for the youngest, City Explorer/Everything for older builders).

Lesson 1 · Grades K–1 · Picture Play mode

🏡 Our First Little Town

Objective: Follow a simple, ordered plan (an algorithm) to build a tiny town where homes connect to a road — practicing sequencing and one-to-one placement.
Vocabulary:
plansteproadhomein orderfirst / next / last
  1. As a class, say the plan aloud: "First a road, next some homes, last a tree." (This is our algorithm.)
  2. Turn on the 🔊 read-aloud speaker so the game names each tool.
  3. Pick the 🛣️ Road button and drag to draw one straight road across the grass.
  4. Tap the 🏠 Homes picture, then tap the map three times right next to the road — count "1, 2, 3" together.
  5. Watch what appears: little people and cars! Ask why they came (the homes).
  6. Finish with the 🌳 Tree tool — plant two trees so the town looks nice.
Talk about it:
  • What did we build first? What came next?
  • What happened after we added homes? Where did the people come from?
  • If we did it out of order — homes with no road — would it still work?
Lesson 2 · Grades 2–3 · City Explorer mode

🌳 Community Helpers & Clean Air

Objective: Place community buildings where they make sense, and use trees and clean energy to keep a factory town's air clean — connecting design choices to an environmental result.
Vocabulary:
communityfire stationparkfactorypollutionclean airwind powerbalance
  1. Start a fresh city and note the 🌿 clean-air meter at the top — it's full and green.
  2. Draw a 🛣️ Road loop, then add 3–4 🏭 Factories from the Factories drawer. Watch the smoke — and the clean-air meter drop.
  3. Predict as a class: "How can we make the air cleaner without erasing the factories?"
  4. Use the 🌳 Tree tool to plant trees right next to each factory, and add a Wind Turbine from the Fun drawer. Watch the meter climb back up.
  5. Now be a city planner: from the 🎡 Fun drawer, place a School, a Fire Station, and a Park where they'd help people most. Watch the 😀 happiness face.
  6. Erase and re-place one building to a better spot if the meters don't improve.
Talk about it:
  • What made the clean-air meter go down? What brought it back up?
  • Why did you put the fire station where you did? Who does it help?
  • How do real towns keep their air clean? (Trees, parks, clean energy.)
Lesson 3 · Grades 3–4 · City Explorer / Everything · pairs

🤝 Build Together: Plan a City With a Partner

Objective: Break a shared goal into parts, divide the work, and iterate on one city together — practicing decomposition and collaborative design.
Vocabulary:
plancollaboratedecomposeneighborhooddowntowncomparerevise
  1. One partner taps 🤝 Build Together to get a room code and reads it to the other, who taps Join a Friend and enters it.
  2. Together, agree on a plan and decompose it: Partner A builds the homes neighborhood; Partner B builds the 🏙️ Downtown towers.
  3. Both use the 🛣️ Road tool to connect your two areas — meet in the middle and cross any river with a bridge.
  4. Add shared community spots — a Park, a School — and discuss where each should go.
  5. Step back and compare: does everything connect? Are people and cars moving? Revise anything that feels crowded or cut off.
  6. Name your city and take a 📷 postcard together to save your teamwork.
Talk about it:
  • How did you split up the work? Why did that division make sense?
  • What did your partner do that you wouldn't have thought of?
  • What would you change if you started your shared city over?

4 Talking With Kids About What They Made

Open-ended prompts beat yes/no questions. Ask children to show you as they explain — it turns their building into storytelling and reflection.

  • Give me a tour. "Walk me through your city — where does the story start?"
  • Purpose. "Why did you put the fire station there? Who does it help?"
  • Cause & effect. "Your happiness face is smiling big — what did you build that made people happy?"
  • Environment. "Your air got a little cloudy. What could you add to help it?"
  • Trade-offs. "You had a mountain and a river in the way. How did you plan around them?"
  • Iteration. "Was there something you built, then changed? What made you change it?"
  • Naming & identity. "You named your city ___ — why that name? What is it famous for?"
  • Collaboration. "What was the best part of building with a partner? The trickiest?"

5 Capstone & Rubric: "Make Your Own City"

The task: Over one or two sessions, plan and build a city that (1) connects its buildings with roads, (2) includes at least one community helper (school, fire station, or park), and (3) keeps its clean-air meter healthy. Then give a short tour explaining your choices.

Look-for 🌱 Beginning 🌿 Growing 🌳 Soaring
Planning & sequence Places buildings one at a time with little order. Follows a rough plan — roads first, then buildings nearby. Describes a clear plan and builds it in a sensible order, adjusting as needed.
Connection (roads) Buildings are scattered; few reach a road. Most buildings touch a road; roads form a simple network. All key buildings connect; roads bridge water and link separate areas.
Community thinking Adds buildings mostly at random. Includes at least one helper (school/fire station/park) in a fitting spot. Places several helpers thoughtfully and can explain who each one serves.
Environment (clean air) Doesn't notice the clean-air meter. Notices pollution and adds some trees or wind power. Deliberately balances factories with trees/clean energy and explains the effect.
Explaining choices Names a few things in the city. Gives reasons for a couple of placements. Tells a clear story of the city and justifies key design decisions.
💡 Make it a keepsake: have each child use the 📷 photo button to name their city and save a postcard, then dictate or write one sentence about it — a natural literacy extension and a ready-made portfolio artifact.
